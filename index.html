Welcome to the Re-Engagement Center Network

In collaboration with public systems that impact the lives of many Opportunity Youth, ages 18-24, who are out of work and out of school, this network of service providers utilizes youth development principles, transformative mentoring relationships, work experience and the meeting of basic needs to re-engage disconnected young adults to education and career pathways.


"We have to address that element of trust and consistency, so we are there to repair the harm the system has done in so many ways."

Paul Flores, Latino Men and Boys Program, Unity Council

What is the Re-Engagement Center Network?

Collaborating with Public Systems

The anticipated impact of the Re-Engagement Center Network (RECN) will only be sustained if we simultaneously work with the many public agencies who touch the lives of opportunity youth, to systematically address barriers and work to build a coordinated and aligned set of support and services. The RECN aims to work closely with public systems including but not limited to the school districts, community college districts, Alameda County Social Services Agency, and Alameda County Probation Department to ensure that young adults who exit education and career pathways have improved, clearly articulated and supported on ramps to education and career pathways.

Key Components

All of the service providers in the network have these five key components in their programming:

  • TARGETED OUTREACH TO DISCONNECTED YOUNG ADULTS
  • CARING ADULTS
  • STABILIZATION SERVICES
  • EMPLOYMENT SERVICES
  • EDUCATION PLANNING AND SUPPORT

Geography and Model Variation

Creating a network of service providers allows for geographic coverage as well as model variation that address the diverse interests and motivation of Opportunity Youth. For some, it’s important to earn a high school diploma, for others, the cultural competency of an organization with deep roots in a neighborhood is compelling, and still for others, career specific pathways may be what draws a young adult to re-engage.

Why Focus on Opportunity Youth?


"If they are not in school, not working...there’s a greater possibility of falling into the public safety area, or having deteriorating health. This can have a destabilizing effect on families and a big community cost, so it’s much better to dedicate resources to the pipeline of cradle to career."

Keith Carson, County Supervisor

Nationwide there are 5.8 million young people, ages 16-24, who are neither in school or working, and 700,000 in California, with 20,000 residing in Alameda County. Their disconnection represents a significant loss of economic opportunity. The direct cost to taxpayers for not re-engaging opportunity youth public benefit dollars is estimated at $13,980 per year, and $235k over a lifetime. Overall, the economic burden from failing to invest in all of America’s youth is substantial.
